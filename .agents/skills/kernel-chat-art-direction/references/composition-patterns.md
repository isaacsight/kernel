# Editorial Composition Patterns

Use these patterns as prompts, not templates. Combine at most two in one issue and change their proportions to match the argument.

## Cover Archetypes

### The Monument

One image or typographic form occupies most of the field. Small metadata establishes scale. Best for a single decisive claim.

### The Evidence Table

One central specimen is surrounded by numbered observations, measurements, or marginal notes. Best for inquiry and close looking.

### The Interrupted Grid

A strict editorial grid is broken once by the subject. Best for tension between system and exception.

### The Field Window

A narrow crop or aperture implies a larger world outside the page. Best for place, travel, ecology, or partial knowledge.

### The Paired Register

Two images, languages, time states, or measurements occupy unequal but related fields. Best for comparison without reducing both sides to symmetry.

## Spread Rhythm

Plan a page as changing editorial pressure:

1. **Threshold:** establish atmosphere and issue identity.
2. **Orientation:** give the reader the claim and reading path.
3. **Compression:** bring text, evidence, or controls into close relation.
4. **Release:** allow one image, quote, or field of negative space to dominate.
5. **Complication:** introduce an exception, second register, or working model.
6. **Resolution:** return to the opening material with changed meaning.

Avoid repeating the same section silhouette more than twice in succession.

## Responsive Transformations

- Convert side annotations into inline marginal breaks placed after the sentence they qualify.
- Turn an image-and-text split into a deliberate sequence: image, observation, claim, evidence.
- Preserve one strong crop rather than shrinking a complex desktop tableau.
- Keep reading order correct in the DOM; use layout, not duplicate content, to change composition.
- Replace hover-dependent discovery with visible labels or tap-safe controls.
- Remove atmosphere before removing meaning.

## Anti-Generic Test

Ask all five questions before implementation:

1. Could the same layout advertise an unrelated software product by changing only the copy?
2. Is the hero built from a centered headline, gradient glow, pill, and generic call-to-action?
3. Are most ideas enclosed in equal rounded cards?
4. Does every section use the same width, alignment, and vertical spacing?
5. Would the issue still be identifiable in grayscale with all body copy blurred?

If the first four answers are yes or the fifth is no, strengthen the editorial concept before coding.
