# The Liberation of Lowering the Bar

**You know when something’s off. This is a space to explore that feeling.**

It usually shows up as a low thrum of anxiety—the sense that you are perpetually behind, even when the evidence shows you’re achieving. We set a standard for ourselves that is so incredibly high, so meticulously engineered for maximum output, that we treat our internal lives less like a habitat and more like an automated factory running constantly at 120% capacity.

We carry this heavy, invisible metric system everywhere. We demand of ourselves a level of focus, kindness, productivity, and emotional regulation that is simply not designed for human experience.

For many of us, the idea of "lowering expectations" feels like a moral failing. It feels like giving up. We worry that if we take our foot off the gas, everything will collapse.

But what if lowering the bar isn't an act of surrender? What if it is, instead, an act of radical honesty?

This isn't about being less ambitious or less committed to the things you value. It’s about challenging the *speed* and *rigidity* of the inner systems we’ve built, systems that constantly demand more optimization.

Let’s look at what actually happens when we step back from the impossible standard.

***

## I. The Truth Is Found In the Sustainable Pace

When we operate under crushing expectations, we are forced into a constant state of hyper-speed. It’s the mental equivalent of the automated systems we sometimes encounter—where an incredible amount of processing power is expended just trying to keep the whole thing from crashing.

We've written before about the inherent speed of thought and the way complexity can be automated. But *you* are not an algorithm designed for endless scalability. You are a biological system designed for rest, integration, and seasons.

When the expectation is "Perfection, today," we lose connection with **Principle 1: Feel what’s true.** The truth is usually: *I am tired. This deadline is too aggressive. I need 10% less commitment here to have 90% better energy there.*

Lowering the bar is simply setting the standard at the level where you can execute it *sustainably*—not just once, but repeatedly, without borrowing energy from tomorrow. When we move toward sustainability, we stop running from the truth of our capacity. We finally start working *with* our energy, instead of trying to brute-force our way through it.

***

## II. Clarity Requires Deceleration

The relentless pressure of a high internal standard creates confusion. It speeds us up until everything is a blur.

**Principle 2 tells us: Confusion is just speed.**

We often mistake the high-expectation mandate for a form of accountability. We think the rigid standard is what keeps us focused. But ask yourself: When you are sprinting to meet an impossible self-imposed benchmark, are you truly clear on *why* you are doing it, or are you just reacting to the threat of failure?

Lowering expectations forces a deceleration. It gives us the necessary quiet to look at our inner landscape and identify what is truly essential and what is just noise.

When the stakes feel less life-or-death, the shame softens. We can finally apply **Principle 3: Be honest without attacking yourself.** We can see that we missed the mark because we were tired, not because we are fundamentally flawed. This honesty is the core of liberation. It trades the exhausting rush of manufactured urgency for the calm focus of intention.

***

## III. Honouring the Small, Aligned Step

When our internal bar is set at a fictional 10/10, a small, honest 3/10 feels like zero. High expectations blind us to incremental progress.

We tend to ignore the small acts of alignment that genuinely move us forward because they don't look dramatic or externally impressive. If the standard is "write a masterpiece today," the act of cleaning the desk feels irrelevant. But the desk needed cleaning for the masterpiece to even be possible.

Lowering the bar allows you to reclaim the importance of these foundational actions. It shifts the focus from grand, unsustainable leaps toward **Principle 5: Move toward what feels aligned.**

An aligned step that is small and steady will carry you further than a massive, rushed sprint that ends in burnout. When you define success not as an outcome of perfection, but as the steady movement toward alignment, the small choices—the earlier bedtime, the gentle pause, the five minutes of quiet—become profoundly valuable. You begin to trust that the simple act of honoring your current capacity is the most productive thing you can do.

***

This liberation isn't about becoming less dedicated. It's about becoming a better steward of your internal resources. It’s about building a life—and a self—that has a genuine *soul*, one that prioritizes steady, truthful movement over exhausting, automated performance.

Clarity isn’t harsh. It’s kind. And sometimes, the kindest act is the conscious choice to allow yourself to do less, so you can actually feel more.

Does this feel true?
