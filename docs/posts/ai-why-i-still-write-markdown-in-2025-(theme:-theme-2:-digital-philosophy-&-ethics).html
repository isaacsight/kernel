Why I Still Write Markdown in 2025: A Stand Against the Ephemeral Web

In a world awash with AI-powered content creation and shimmering, interactive websites built with frameworks I can barely pronounce, you might wonder why I'm still here, stubbornly pecking away at a plain text file in Markdown. The answer, as always, lies not just in efficiency, but in a deeper philosophical commitment to the long-term health and accessibility of the digital world. I believe Markdown represents a small, but vital, act of digital resistance against the forces that threaten to make our digital history unreadable and our online experiences fleeting.

1. Data Ownership and the Ethics of Lock-in

It’s 2025. Remember that amazing blogging platform everyone loved back in '23? The one with the drag-and-drop interface and the AI-powered SEO optimization? Yeah, they got acquired. Now all that beautiful content is locked behind a subscription wall or, worse, has been silently migrated to a new platform where all the formatting is broken and your carefully crafted images are missing.

This, friends, is the ethics of lock-in. We’ve become so accustomed to relying on proprietary platforms that we've ceded control over our own data. Markdown, in its stark simplicity, is a rejection of this. My content, stored in easily readable text files, can be migrated anywhere. I'm not beholden to any platform, any algorithm, any corporate whim. I own my words, and I can take them with me. This isn't just about convenience; it's about digital sovereignty. It's about refusing to be a serf in someone else's digital kingdom.

2. Preservation Against the Obsolescence Machine

The digital world is a hungry beast, constantly demanding the newest, the shiniest, the most interactive. But what happens to the old? Too often, it's forgotten, abandoned, rendered inaccessible. The websites of the early 2000s, with their Flash animations and Geocities aesthetics, are largely lost to time, relics only accessible through fragmented archives.

Markdown, however, is built to last. Because it's plain text, it's inherently resistant to obsolescence. As long as computers can read text files (and I'm betting they will for the foreseeable future), my Markdown files will be accessible. The tools I use to edit and publish them may change, the presentation may evolve, but the core content remains intact. This is crucial for preserving our digital history. We owe it to future generations to leave behind a record that is readable and accessible, not a collection of broken links and proprietary file formats. Markdown offers a pragmatic, ethical solution to the ever-present threat of digital decay.

3. Accessibility and the Democratization of Content Creation

In a world increasingly dominated by sophisticated design tools and AI-powered writing assistants, it's easy to forget that many people lack the resources or skills to participate fully in the digital conversation. Markdown, with its simple syntax and readily available editors, offers a low barrier to entry for content creation.

You don't need a powerful computer or expensive software to write in Markdown. You don't need to be a coding expert or a design guru. All you need is a text editor and a willingness to learn a few basic commands. This democratization of content creation is essential for ensuring that diverse voices are heard and that the digital landscape reflects the richness and complexity of human experience. By choosing Markdown, I'm not just choosing a writing format; I'm choosing to support a more equitable and accessible digital world.

In conclusion, while the siren song of fancy frameworks and AI-generated content may be tempting, I remain committed to Markdown. It's a small act of resistance, a declaration of digital independence, and a commitment to a more sustainable and accessible digital future. And besides, it just works.
