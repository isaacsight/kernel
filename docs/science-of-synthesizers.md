## 1. PHYSICS OF SOUND SYNTHESIS

### 1.1 How Oscillators Generate Waveforms

An oscillator produces a periodic voltage that repeats at a given frequency. In analog circuits, this is typically achieved through feedback networks: a capacitor charges and discharges through resistors or active components, generating a repeating voltage pattern. In digital systems, the oscillator is a mathematical function evaluated at each sample: a phase accumulator increments by `delta_phase = f / f_s` each sample (where `f` is the desired frequency and `f_s` is the sample rate), and the accumulated phase is used to look up or compute the instantaneous waveform amplitude.

The four fundamental waveforms are:

**Sine wave**: The purest tone, containing only the fundamental frequency. Mathematically:

    x(t) = A * sin(2 * pi * f * t)

In analog circuits, sine waves are generated by resonant LC or RC oscillator circuits (Wien bridge, phase-shift oscillators). Digitally, they are computed via lookup tables or the `sin()` function applied to the phase accumulator.

**Sawtooth wave**: Contains all integer harmonics (both odd and even), with amplitudes that decrease as 1/n where n is the harmonic number. Its Fourier series is:

    x(t) = (2A / pi) * SUM[n=1 to inf] ( (-1)^(n+1) * sin(2*pi*n*f*t) / n )

In analog circuits, a sawtooth is typically generated by linearly charging a capacitor with a constant current source, then rapidly discharging it through a transistor switch (the "reset"), producing the characteristic ramp-and-drop shape.

**Square wave**: Contains only odd harmonics (1, 3, 5, 7...) with amplitudes decreasing as 1/n. Its Fourier series is:

    x(t) = (4A / pi) * SUM[n=1,3,5...] ( sin(2*pi*n*f*t) / n )

Analog square waves are generated by comparator circuits or Schmitt triggers that switch between two voltage rails. The output is either +V or -V with no intermediate values.

**Triangle wave**: Contains only odd harmonics like the square wave, but amplitudes decrease as 1/n^2, making it much softer. Its Fourier series is:

    x(t) = (8A / pi^2) * SUM[n=1,3,5...] ( (-1)^((n-1)/2) * sin(2*pi*n*f*t) / n^2 )

In analog circuits, triangles are generated by integrating a square wave -- a capacitor charged by a constant current alternating in direction.

### 1.2 Fourier Series and Additive Synthesis Theory

Jean-Baptiste Fourier demonstrated in 1822 that any periodic function can be decomposed into (or constructed from) a sum of sinusoidal components. This is the foundation of additive synthesis.

A periodic signal with fundamental frequency f_0 can be expressed as:

    x(t) = a_0/2 + SUM[n=1 to inf] ( a_n * cos(2*pi*n*f_0*t) + b_n * sin(2*pi*n*f_0*t) )

where the Fourier coefficients are:

    a_n = (2/T) * INTEGRAL[0 to T] x(t) * cos(2*pi*n*f_0*t) dt
    b_n = (2/T) * INTEGRAL[0 to T] x(t) * sin(2*pi*n*f_0*t) dt

In additive synthesis, each harmonic is generated by a separate oscillator with independent amplitude and phase control. The Hammond organ (1935) is a classic electromechanical additive synthesizer using tonewheels. Digital additive synthesis (as in the Kawai K5000) gives precise control over hundreds of partials but is computationally expensive.

### 1.3 Why Different Waveforms Have Different Harmonic Content

The harmonic content of a waveform is determined by its geometric symmetry:

- **Half-wave symmetry** (the signal inverted and shifted by half a period equals itself): eliminates all even harmonics. Both square and triangle waves possess this property, which is why they contain only odd harmonics.

- **Discontinuities** (jumps in the waveform): produce harmonics that roll off slowly (1/n). The sawtooth has one discontinuity per period; the square wave has two.

- **Discontinuities in the derivative** (corners rather than jumps): produce harmonics that roll off faster (1/n^2). The triangle wave has corners but no jumps, hence its 1/n^2 rolloff.

- **Smooth signals**: A sine wave has no discontinuities of any order, so it contains only a single frequency component.

The general rule: the sharper the feature in the time domain, the more high-frequency energy is needed in the frequency domain to represent it. This is a direct consequence of the uncertainty principle applied to signal analysis.

### 1.4 The Relationship Between Harmonics and Timbre

Timbre -- the quality that distinguishes a trumpet from a violin playing the same note at the same loudness -- is primarily determined by:

1. **The relative amplitudes of harmonics**: A clarinet emphasizes odd harmonics (similar to a square wave); a violin has a rich mix of odd and even harmonics decreasing gradually.

2. **The temporal evolution of harmonics**: In acoustic instruments, each harmonic has its own amplitude envelope. Higher harmonics typically decay faster than lower ones, which is why a piano note becomes "duller" as it sustains.

3. **Inharmonic partials**: Real instruments produce partials that are not exact integer multiples of the fundamental. A piano string's partials are slightly stretched (sharp) due to string stiffness. Bells and metallic percussion produce strongly inharmonic spectra.

4. **Formant regions**: Fixed resonances of a body (vocal tract, violin body) amplify certain frequency bands regardless of the fundamental pitch, creating characteristic timbral "fingerprints."

## 2. SUBTRACTIVE SYNTHESIS SCIENCE

### 2.1 How Analog Filters Work

Subtractive synthesis starts with a harmonically rich waveform (typically sawtooth or pulse) and removes harmonics using voltage-controlled filters (VCFs). All analog filters are built from combinations of resistors (R), capacitors (C), inductors (L), and active components (transistors, op-amps).

The fundamental unit is the first-order RC lowpass filter:

    H(s) = 1 / (1 + s*R*C)

where s = j*omega is the complex frequency variable. The cutoff frequency is:

    f_c = 1 / (2 * pi * R * C)

Above f_c, attenuation increases at 6 dB/octave (20 dB/decade) -- this is a single "pole."

**Transistor ladder filter (Moog)**: Patented by Robert Moog in 1966 (US Patent 3,475,623). Four cascaded differential transistor pairs form four first-order sections. Each transistor pair acts as a voltage-controlled resistor: varying the bias current through the transistors changes the effective resistance, which shifts the cutoff frequency. The four poles produce a 24 dB/octave slope. A global negative feedback path from the output back to the input creates resonance. At a feedback gain of 4, the phase shift through the four stages totals 180 degrees at the cutoff frequency, satisfying the Barkhausen criterion for oscillation -- the filter begins to self-oscillate, producing a pure sine wave at the cutoff frequency.

The Moog ladder's transfer function (without feedback) is:

    H(s) = 1 / (1 + s/omega_c)^4

With feedback gain k (0 to 4):

    H(s) = 1 / ( (1 + s/omega_c)^4 + k )

**State-variable filter (SVF)**: Uses integrators (op-amps with capacitive feedback) connected in a loop. The SVF simultaneously provides lowpass, highpass, bandpass, and notch (band-reject) outputs from a single circuit. The Oberheim SEM (1974) is the classic SVF synthesizer. Its 12 dB/octave slope (two poles) gives a gentler, more "open" sound than the Moog ladder. Resonance does not cause the volume drop associated with the Moog topology, because the feedback path is structured differently.

**Diode ladder filter**: Replaces the Moog's transistors with diodes. The EMS VCS3 and Roland TB-303 use diode ladder variants. Because diodes lack the buffering effect of transistors between filter stages, the poles interact more strongly. This interaction causes the resonance peak to shift the effective pole positions, creating a different, often more aggressive resonance character.

### 2.2 Why Moog Filters Sound Different from Korg MS-20 Filters

The sonic difference between these filters comes down to circuit topology and the resulting nonlinear behavior:

**Moog transistor ladder (24 dB/oct)**:
- Four matched transistor pairs with resistor biasing that isolates each filter section from the next.
- Resonance is achieved through negative feedback from the fourth stage back to the first.
- As resonance increases, bass frequencies are attenuated (the "bass loss" or "bass robbery" problem) because the feedback signal subtracts from the input at all frequencies below the cutoff.
- Saturation is gradual and symmetrical (transistor soft-clipping), producing predominantly odd harmonics.
- The sound is described as "fat," "round," and "warm."

**Korg MS-20 filter (12 dB/oct lowpass + 6 dB/oct highpass)**:
- The MS-20 actually has two filters in series: a highpass (Sallen-Key topology in the original, OTA-based in later revisions) followed by a lowpass.
- The lowpass section uses a different OTA (operational transconductance amplifier) topology with a bandpass filter in the resonance feedback loop.
- Resonance does not cause bass loss because the feedback architecture differs from the Moog's subtractive approach.
- The circuit saturates asymmetrically and more aggressively at lower input levels, producing both even and odd harmonics.
- The sound is described as "aggressive," "screamy," and "dirty."
- The MS-20's filter self-oscillates with a different character -- more distorted and unstable than the Moog's clean sine-wave self-oscillation.

**Steiner-Parker filter (12 dB/oct)**: Used in the Synthacon and later the Arturia MiniBrute. A Sallen-Key derived topology that switches between lowpass, highpass, and bandpass by changing which components the input signal feeds. Uses positive feedback for resonance, so resonance does not reduce output volume. Tends toward chaotic, unpredictable behavior at extreme settings.

### 2.3 Resonance and Self-Oscillation Physics

Resonance in a filter is created by feeding a portion of the output back to the input, creating a feedback loop that amplifies frequencies near the cutoff. The physics:

1. At the cutoff frequency, the filter's phase shift approaches the value needed for positive feedback (180 degrees for a 4-pole inverting feedback path, 360 degrees for a non-inverting path).

2. When the feedback gain equals or exceeds the filter's attenuation at the cutoff frequency, the Barkhausen stability criterion is met: the loop gain >= 1 and the phase shift = n * 360 degrees. The filter becomes an oscillator.

3. In a self-oscillating filter, the amplitude is limited by the nonlinear saturation of the active components (transistors or op-amps clipping). This is why analog self-oscillation has character -- the saturation waveshaping adds harmonics to the oscillation.

4. The Q factor (quality factor) of the resonance is: Q = f_c / bandwidth. Higher Q means a narrower, taller resonance peak. In synthesizer terminology, "resonance" and "emphasis" refer to the Q control. Typical ranges go from Q = 0.707 (Butterworth, maximally flat) to Q > 100 (near self-oscillation).

### 2.4 Filter Slope Mathematics

Each "pole" of a filter contributes 6 dB/octave (equivalently, 20 dB/decade) of attenuation in the stopband:

| Poles | Slope (dB/oct) | Slope (dB/dec) | Common Name | Character |
|-------|----------------|----------------|-------------|-----------|
| 1 | 6 | 20 | 1-pole | Gentle, subtle |
| 2 | 12 | 40 | 2-pole | Moderate, SEM-like |
| 3 | 18 | 60 | 3-pole | Uncommon, TB-303 HPF |
| 4 | 24 | 80 | 4-pole | Classic Moog |
| 8 | 48 | 160 | 8-pole | Extreme, brickwall-like |

The transfer function of an n-pole Butterworth lowpass is:

    |H(j*omega)|^2 = 1 / (1 + (omega/omega_c)^(2n))

At one octave above cutoff (omega = 2 * omega_c), the attenuation for 4 poles is:

    20 * log10(1 / sqrt(1 + 2^8)) = 20 * log10(1/16) = -24.08 dB

This confirms the 24 dB/octave slope.

## 3. FM SYNTHESIS

### 3.1 The Mathematics of Frequency Modulation

FM synthesis was discovered for musical applications by John Chowning at Stanford's CCRMA in 1967 (published 1973). The basic FM equation is:

    x(t) = A * sin(2*pi*f_c*t + I * sin(2*pi*f_m*t))

where:
- f_c = carrier frequency
- f_m = modulator frequency
- I = modulation index (also called beta) = delta_f / f_m
- delta_f = peak frequency deviation

The modulation index I determines the bandwidth and spectral richness. As I increases from 0, the carrier's energy is redistributed into an increasing number of sidebands.

Using the Jacobi-Anger expansion, this signal can be decomposed:

    x(t) = A * SUM[n=-inf to inf] J_n(I) * sin(2*pi*(f_c + n*f_m)*t)

where J_n(I) is the **Bessel function of the first kind, order n**, evaluated at the modulation index.

Key properties of Bessel functions in FM:
- J_0(I): amplitude of the carrier component
- J_1(I): amplitude of the first upper and lower sidebands at f_c +/- f_m
- J_n(I): amplitude of the nth sideband pair at f_c +/- n*f_m
- J_{-n}(I) = (-1)^n * J_n(I): odd-order lower sidebands are phase-inverted
- For small I (< 0.5): only J_0 and J_1 are significant (two sidebands, similar to AM)
- For large I (> 5): many sidebands are significant, producing a bright, complex spectrum
- When J_0(I) = 0 (at I approximately 2.405, 5.520, 8.654...): the carrier component vanishes entirely

The total power is conserved:

    SUM[n=-inf to inf] J_n(I)^2 = 1

### 3.2 Why FM Creates Metallic/Bell Tones

When the carrier-to-modulator frequency ratio (C:M) is not a simple integer ratio, the resulting sidebands are **inharmonic** -- they do not fall on integer multiples of a fundamental. This inharmonicity is precisely the spectral characteristic of struck metal objects (bells, chimes, metallophones).

For example, a C:M ratio of 1:1.4 produces sidebands at frequencies that are not harmonically related. The ear perceives this as "metallic" or "bell-like." In contrast, C:M = 1:1 produces all harmonics (like a sawtooth), and C:M = 1:2 produces only odd harmonics (like a square wave).

The spectral density and rapid amplitude variation of FM sidebands across the spectrum also contribute to the metallic quality -- real bells have dozens of inharmonic partials with complex decay patterns, and FM naturally produces this type of spectrum.

### 3.3 Carrier/Modulator Ratios and Their Harmonic Results

| C:M Ratio | Harmonic Structure | Musical Character |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| 1:1 | All harmonics (f, 2f, 3f, 4f...) | Sawtooth-like, brass |
| 1:2 | Odd harmonics (f, 3f, 5f...) | Square-wave-like, clarinet |
| 1:3 | Every 3rd harmonic enhanced | Hollow, woody |
| 2:1 | Even harmonics emphasized | Octave-shifted buzz |
| 1:1.414 | Inharmonic | Bell, chime |
| 1:pi | Strongly inharmonic | Metallic, gong |
| 1:0 | No modulation | Pure sine tone |

When the C:M ratio uses integer values, the resulting spectrum is harmonic with a fundamental equal to the GCD of f_c and f_m. Non-integer ratios produce inharmonic spectra with a perceived pitch ambiguity characteristic of percussion.

### 3.4 DX7 Algorithm Architecture

The Yamaha DX7 (1983) uses 6 operators (sine-wave oscillators with individual amplitude envelopes) arranged in 32 fixed routing configurations called **algorithms**. Each operator can function as either a carrier (audible output) or a modulator (modifies another operator's frequency).

Key architectural features:
- **6 operators per voice**, 16-voice polyphony (96 oscillators total)
- **32 algorithms**: ranging from simple 2-operator stacks to complex 6-way parallel configurations
- **Feedback**: exactly one operator per algorithm has a self-feedback path, where its output is routed back to its own input. Increasing feedback progressively changes the operator's output from sine toward sawtooth.
- **Frequency ratios**: each operator can be set to integer ratios (1x, 2x, 3x... up to 31x) or fractional "fixed frequency" mode for inharmonic effects.
- **8-stage envelope generators**: each operator has its own rate/level envelope (4 rates, 4 levels), providing more complex amplitude shaping than typical ADSR envelopes.
- **Velocity sensitivity**: each operator responds to key velocity independently, allowing dynamic spectral control.

Algorithm 1 (the most basic): operators 6->5->4->3->2->1, a single modulator chain where each operator modulates the next. Operator 1 is the sole carrier.

Algorithm 32 (the most parallel): all 6 operators output directly as carriers with no modulation -- pure additive synthesis of six sine waves.

## 4. WAVETABLE SYNTHESIS

### 4.1 How Wavetable Interpolation Works

Wavetable synthesis stores single cycles of waveforms in memory and reads through them at variable rates to produce different pitches. A wavetable is a one-dimensional array of N samples representing one period.

**Phase accumulator method**: A floating-point phase value increments by `delta = f * N / f_s` each sample. The integer part indexes the table; the fractional part is used for interpolation between adjacent samples.

Interpolation methods (in order of increasing quality):
1. **Nearest-neighbor (zero-order hold)**: No interpolation. Produces quantization noise.
2. **Linear**: Weighted average of two adjacent samples. Simple but introduces low-frequency ripple.
3. **Cubic/Hermite**: Uses four neighboring samples. Much smoother, standard in most synths.
4. **Sinc interpolation**: Theoretically perfect reconstruction but computationally expensive.

**Wavetable morphing**: Multiple single-cycle waveforms are stored in a 2D array (the "wavetable"). A position parameter crossfades between adjacent frames. Morphing can occur in the time domain (simple crossfade) or the spectral domain (interpolating FFT magnitudes and phases separately).

### 4.2 Anti-Aliasing in Digital Oscillators

The fundamental problem: classic waveforms (saw, square, pulse) have theoretically infinite bandwidth. When generated digitally at a finite sample rate, harmonics above the Nyquist frequency (f_s/2) fold back into the audible range as aliasing artifacts -- inharmonic, unmusical frequencies.

**Naive (trivial) oscillator**: Computes the mathematical waveform directly. At MIDI note C5 (523 Hz) with f_s = 44100 Hz, a sawtooth has harmonics up to n = 42 below Nyquist. But at C7 (2093 Hz), only n = 10 harmonics fit. The aliased harmonics above Nyquist fold back down, creating audible artifacts especially in the upper register.

**Bandlimited Impulse Train (BLIT)**: Developed by Stilson & Smith at CCRMA (1996). Generates an impulse train using a windowed sinc function that naturally contains no energy above Nyquist. Classic waveforms are derived by integration:
- Unipolar BLIT -> integrate -> sawtooth
- Bipolar BLIT -> integrate -> square wave
- Bipolar BLIT -> integrate twice -> triangle wave

The BLIT itself is computed as:

    BLIT(t) = (M / P) * sin(pi * M * t / P) / sin(pi * t / P)

where M is the number of harmonics and P is the period in samples.

**MinBLEP (minimum-phase Band-Limited Step)**: Instead of generating the entire waveform bandlimited, minBLEP computes a pre-computed bandlimited step function and inserts it at each discontinuity of the naive waveform. The minBLEP residual (the difference between the ideal step and the bandlimited step) is added to the naive output. This is computationally efficient and widely used in modern soft synths.

**Oversampling**: Generate the waveform at 2x, 4x, or 8x the output sample rate, then apply a steep lowpass filter and downsample. This pushes aliasing artifacts above the audible range. It works but is computationally expensive and does not fully eliminate aliasing -- it only attenuates it by the stopband rejection of the decimation filter.

**Wavetable mip-mapping**: For wavetable oscillators specifically, pre-compute a set of bandlimited versions of each waveform at multiple octave ranges (similar to texture mip-maps in 3D graphics). At runtime, select the table appropriate for the current pitch. This is extremely efficient and produces excellent results. This is the approach Xfer Serum uses.

### 4.3 Why Serum Sounds Cleaner Than Older Wavetable Synths

Serum (Xfer Records, 2014) represented a generational leap in wavetable synthesis quality for several reasons:

1. **Spectral wavetable processing**: Serum stores and manipulates wavetables in the frequency domain (FFT), allowing clean bandlimiting at every pitch by simply zeroing harmonics above Nyquist before inverse-FFT. Older synths like the PPG Wave and Waldorf Microwave operated purely in the time domain with fixed-rate tables, producing audible aliasing at higher pitches.

2. **High-resolution tables**: Serum uses 2048-sample wavetable frames (vs. 256 or 512 in older hardware). More samples = more precise waveform representation and cleaner interpolation.

3. **4x internal oversampling**: Combined with spectral bandlimiting for a belt-and-suspenders approach.

4. **Sub-sample accurate phase tracking**: Prevents timing jitter that causes spectral smearing.

5. **Clean morphing**: Spectral-domain interpolation between wavetable frames avoids the click artifacts of time-domain crossfading.

### 4.4 Spectral vs. Time-Domain Wavetable Morphing

**Time-domain morphing**: Simple crossfade between waveform samples. Fast but can produce click artifacts at discontinuity boundaries and creates intermediate waveforms that may not be spectrally meaningful (two peaks can merge into a wider peak rather than sweeping smoothly).

**Spectral-domain morphing**: Each wavetable frame is stored as its FFT (magnitude + phase). Morphing interpolates magnitudes and phases (or magnitudes only, with phase reconstruction) independently. Advantages:
- Smooth harmonic transitions without time-domain artifacts
- Bandlimiting is trivial (zero coefficients above Nyquist)
- Formant preservation possible by interpolating in a warped frequency scale

The computational cost is higher (requires FFT/IFFT per frame transition), but modern CPUs handle this easily.

## 5. GRANULAR SYNTHESIS

### 5.1 The Physics of Granular Decomposition

Granular synthesis operates on the principle that any sound can be decomposed into, or constructed from, a cloud of very short sonic events called **grains**. This concept traces to Dennis Gabor's 1947 paper "Acoustical Quanta and the Theory of Hearing," where he proposed that sound perception occurs in discrete temporal quanta of approximately 20-50 ms.

A grain is a brief waveform segment (typically 1-100 ms) multiplied by an amplitude envelope (usually a Gaussian, Hanning, or raised-cosine window). Mathematically, a single grain is:

    g(t) = w(t) * s(t)

where w(t) is the grain envelope (window function) and s(t) is the source waveform within the grain.

The output of a granular synthesizer is the sum of many overlapping grains:

    y(t) = SUM[i=1 to N] a_i * w(t - t_i) * s_i(t - t_i)

where a_i is the amplitude, t_i is the onset time, and s_i is the source waveform of the ith grain.

### 5.2 Grain Density, Size, and Overlap Mathematics

**Grain density** (grains per second): Determines the texture of the output. At low densities (1-10 grains/sec), individual grains are perceived as separate events. Above approximately 15-20 grains/sec, grains fuse into a continuous texture. Densities of 100-1000+ grains/sec create dense, cloud-like sounds.

**Grain size and bandwidth**: There is an inverse relationship between grain duration and spectral bandwidth, directly following from the time-frequency uncertainty principle:

    delta_t * delta_f >= 1 / (4 * pi)

Short grains (1-5 ms) have wide bandwidth -- they sound like clicks with broad spectral content. Longer grains (50-100 ms) preserve more of the source's pitch and spectral characteristics. Gabor's "acoustical quantum" of approximately 25 ms represents a perceptual sweet spot.

**Overlap ratio**: The ratio of grain duration to inter-onset time. An overlap of 1.0 means grains are placed end-to-end; overlap > 1.0 means grains overlap in time (typical values 2-8). Higher overlap produces smoother output but requires more computation. The envelope shape must be designed so overlapping grains sum to a constant amplitude -- this is the "constant overlap-add" (COLA) constraint:

    SUM[n] w(t - n*H) = 1 for all t

where H is the hop size (inter-onset interval).

### 5.3 Time-Stretching Without Pitch Change

This is one of granular synthesis's most powerful applications. The principle:

1. Extract grains from the source at their original positions.
2. Space the grains further apart in time (stretch) or closer together (compress).
3. Because each grain preserves the original waveform and pitch content, the pitch remains unchanged even though the overall duration changes.

For a stretch factor of S:
- Read position advances by `hop_in = grain_size / overlap_factor` in the source
- Output position advances by `hop_out = hop_in * S`
- When S > 1: grains are spaced further apart, stretching the sound
- When S < 1: grains overlap more, compressing the sound

Artifacts arise when grain boundaries cause phase discontinuities. Advanced algorithms (such as Elastique, used in Ableton Live) use pitch-synchronous grain sizes and cross-correlation to find optimal splice points.

### 5.4 Curtis Roads' Microsound Theory

In *Microsound* (MIT Press, 2001), Curtis Roads establishes a taxonomy of nine time scales in music:

| Time Scale | Duration | Examples |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| Infinite | > hours | Drone installations |
| Supra | minutes to hours | Movements, pieces |
| Macro | seconds to minutes | Phrases, sections |
| Meso | hundreds of ms to seconds | Notes, rhythmic cells |
| Sound object | 100 ms - several seconds | Individual events |
| Micro | < 100 ms | Grains, wavelets |
| Sample | 1/f_s (~23 microsec at 44.1 kHz) | Individual digital samples |
| Subsample | < 1/f_s | Jitter, interpolation |
| Infinitesimal | -> 0 | Dirac impulse (theoretical) |

Roads argues that the **micro** time scale is the most musically under-explored and offers unique creative possibilities. At this scale, the distinction between pitch and rhythm dissolves -- a grain repeated at 50 Hz is simultaneously a rhythm (50 beats per second) and a pitch (50 Hz fundamental). This is the "particle/wave duality" of sound.

## 6. SPECTRAL SYNTHESIS

### 6.1 FFT/STFT Analysis-Resynthesis

The **Fast Fourier Transform (FFT)** converts a block of N time-domain samples into N/2+1 complex frequency bins (for real-valued signals), each representing the magnitude and phase of a frequency component:

    X[k] = SUM[n=0 to N-1] x[n] * e^(-j*2*pi*k*n/N)

The **Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT)** applies the FFT to overlapping windowed segments of a signal, producing a time-frequency representation (spectrogram). Parameters:

- **Window size (N)**: Determines frequency resolution (delta_f = f_s / N). Larger N = better frequency resolution but worse time resolution.
- **Hop size (H)**: The stride between consecutive windows. Typical values: N/4 (75% overlap) or N/2 (50% overlap).
- **Window function**: Hann, Hamming, Blackman-Harris. Determines the tradeoff between main-lobe width (frequency resolution) and side-lobe level (spectral leakage).

**Analysis-resynthesis pipeline**:
1. Analyze: Apply windowed FFT to each frame -> magnitude and phase spectra
2. Modify: Alter magnitudes, phases, or both in the frequency domain
3. Resynthesize: Apply inverse FFT (IFFT) to each modified frame
4. Overlap-add: Sum the overlapping output frames to reconstruct the continuous signal

### 6.2 Phase Vocoder Theory

The phase vocoder (Flanagan & Golden, 1966; Dolson, 1986) extends the STFT to enable independent time and pitch manipulation. The key insight is tracking the **instantaneous frequency** of each bin across frames using phase differences.

For a bin k between frames m and m+1:

    instantaneous_frequency[k] = (phase[m+1][k] - phase[m][k]) / (2*pi*H/f_s)

Time stretching with the phase vocoder:
1. Analyze at the original hop size H_a
2. Resynthesize with a different hop size H_s = H_a * stretch_factor
3. Reconstruct phases by accumulating the instantaneous frequency information at the new hop rate

The phase vocoder avoids the grain-boundary artifacts of simple granular time-stretching but can produce "phasiness" (smeared transients) because the phase coherence between bins is disrupted during modification.

### 6.3 Spectral Processing Techniques

**Spectral freeze**: Capture a single STFT frame and repeat it indefinitely, creating a sustained "frozen" version of a moment in the sound. The magnitudes are held constant; phases are either frozen (producing a static, metallic result) or randomized each frame (producing a smoother, evolving frozen texture).

**Spectral blur**: Average the magnitudes across several consecutive frames, smoothing rapid spectral changes. Creates a "smeared" or reverberant quality.

**Spectral shift**: Translate all frequency bins up or down by a constant amount (not a ratio -- this is frequency shifting, not pitch shifting). Destroys harmonic relationships, creating inharmonic, ring-modulation-like effects.

**Cross-synthesis**: Apply the magnitude spectrum of one sound (the "filter") to the phase spectrum of another (the "excitation"). Classic example: imposing speech formants onto instrumental sounds, making a guitar "talk."

**Spectral gating**: Zero out bins below a magnitude threshold, keeping only the loudest spectral components. The inverse (zeroing bins above a threshold) isolates the noise residual.

## 7. MODULATION SCIENCE

### 7.1 LFO Mathematics and Audio-Rate Effects

A Low-Frequency Oscillator generates a periodic control signal, typically below 20 Hz. The mathematical form is identical to an audio oscillator:

    LFO(t) = A * waveform(2*pi*f_LFO*t + phi)

where f_LFO is the LFO rate, A is the modulation depth, and phi is the phase.

When routed to pitch: vibrato. When routed to amplitude: tremolo. When routed to filter cutoff: "wah" or filter sweep effects.

**Audio-rate LFO behavior**: When f_LFO exceeds approximately 20 Hz and enters the audio range, the modulation ceases to be perceived as a cyclic variation and instead creates new frequency components (sidebands). Specifically:

- LFO -> amplitude = **amplitude modulation (AM)**: produces sidebands at f_carrier +/- f_LFO
- LFO -> pitch/frequency = **frequency modulation (FM)**: produces sidebands at f_carrier +/- n*f_LFO (governed by Bessel functions)
- LFO -> filter cutoff = **dynamic filtering**: at audio rates, creates a comb-filter-like effect with time-varying spectral notches

This is the fundamental connection between modulation and synthesis: at sub-audio rates, modulation creates movement; at audio rates, it creates new harmonics.

### 7.2 Envelope Generator Circuits

The ADSR (Attack-Decay-Sustain-Release) envelope generator is built around capacitor charge/discharge circuits:

**Analog implementation**: A capacitor is charged through a resistor toward a target voltage. The voltage follows an exponential curve:

    V(t) = V_target * (1 - e^(-t / tau))  [charging]
    V(t) = V_initial * e^(-t / tau)         [discharging]

where tau = R * C is the time constant. After one time constant, the voltage has reached 63.2% of its target (charging) or decayed to 36.8% (discharging). After 5 time constants, the voltage is within 0.7% of its target.

In a typical ADSR circuit:
- **Attack**: Capacitor charges toward V_max through R_attack. The attack time is proportional to R_attack * C.
- **Decay**: When V reaches V_max (detected by a comparator), the circuit switches to discharge toward V_sustain through R_decay.
- **Sustain**: A voltage divider or DAC sets V_sustain. The capacitor holds this level while the gate signal is high.
- **Release**: When the gate drops, the capacitor discharges toward 0V through R_release.

### 7.3 Why Exponential Envelopes Sound More Natural Than Linear

Exponential envelopes match how we perceive loudness and pitch:

1. **Weber-Fechner Law**: Human perception of stimulus intensity is approximately logarithmic. Equal ratios of physical intensity produce equal intervals of perceived loudness. An exponential voltage curve applied to a VCA (which typically has an exponential voltage-to-gain response) produces a linear change in perceived loudness.

2. **Acoustic instrument behavior**: The energy decay of vibrating strings, drumheads, and air columns follows exponential curves because energy dissipation is proportional to the current energy level (first-order differential equation: dE/dt = -k*E, solution: E(t) = E_0 * e^(-kt)).

3. **Perceived "snappiness"**: Exponential attack curves change rapidly at first then slow down, reaching the target with a "snap" that matches the initial burst of energy in plucked/struck instruments. Linear envelopes feel sluggish by comparison because they distribute the change evenly over time.

The difference is significant: a linear 100 ms attack sounds slower than an exponential 100 ms attack because the exponential reaches 63% of its target in just ~37 ms.

### 7.4 Ring Modulation: Sum/Difference Frequency Math

Ring modulation multiplies two signals:

    y(t) = x_1(t) * x_2(t)

Using the trigonometric product-to-sum identity:

    sin(A) * sin(B) = 0.5 * [cos(A - B) - cos(A + B)]

So ring-modulating two sine waves at frequencies f_1 and f_2 produces:

    y(t) = 0.5 * [cos(2*pi*(f_1 - f_2)*t) - cos(2*pi*(f_1 + f_2)*t)]

The output contains **only** the sum (f_1 + f_2) and difference (f_1 - f_2) frequencies -- the original frequencies are **suppressed**. This is why it is called a "double-balanced" mixer.

**Amplitude modulation** differs: the modulator is offset to be unipolar (always positive), so the carrier frequency is preserved along with the sidebands:

    y(t) = [1 + m * sin(2*pi*f_m*t)] * sin(2*pi*f_c*t)

This produces three components: f_c, f_c + f_m, and f_c - f_m.

With complex signals (not pure sines), ring modulation produces sum and difference frequencies for **every pair** of partials, creating dense, often inharmonic spectra. This is why ring modulation sounds "metallic" or "robotic."

## 8. PSYCHOACOUSTICS

### 8.1 Why Filter Sweeps Feel Satisfying

The perceptual pleasure of a resonant filter sweep involves multiple psychoacoustic mechanisms:

1. **Spectral flux and novelty detection**: The auditory system responds strongly to spectral change. A sweeping filter continuously introduces new frequency components and removes others, maintaining the listener's attention through constant novelty. The dopaminergic reward system responds to patterns that are predictable yet varied -- a smooth filter sweep provides exactly this balance.

2. **Formant simulation**: Moving resonant peaks across the spectrum mimics the vowel formants of the human voice (approximately 300-3000 Hz). We are neurologically tuned to respond to vocal-like spectral shapes.

3. **Anticipation and resolution**: A filter opening from a muffled state to full brightness creates a sense of building tension and release -- a fundamental principle of musical affect. The exponential nature of most filter sweeps (more change per unit time at the end) mirrors natural crescendo patterns.

### 8.2 Critical Bands and Masking

The cochlea performs a frequency-to-place mapping along the basilar membrane. The **critical band** (or auditory filter) is the frequency bandwidth within which two tones interact perceptually. Two tones within the same critical band produce sensations of roughness and beating; they also mask each other (one tone makes the other harder to hear).

Critical bandwidth is approximately:

    CB(f) = 25 + 75 * (1 + 1.4 * (f/1000)^2)^0.69  [Hz]

(Approximation from Zwicker & Terhardt, 1980)

At low frequencies (~100 Hz), the critical band is approximately 100 Hz wide. At 1 kHz, it is approximately 160 Hz. At 10 kHz, approximately 2500 Hz.

The **Bark scale** (named after Heinrich Barkhausen) divides the audible range into 24 critical bands, each approximately 1 Bark wide. This scale is fundamental to perceptual audio coding (MP3, AAC) and explains why certain intervals sound "rough" (minor seconds at low frequencies, where the notes fall within one critical band) or "smooth" (the same interval at high frequencies, where critical bands are wider).

**Masking**: A louder tone masks nearby quieter tones within the same critical band. This occurs both **simultaneously** (simultaneous masking) and **temporally** (forward masking: a loud sound masks softer sounds for 50-200 ms after it ends; backward masking: a loud sound can mask softer sounds up to 20 ms before it).

### 8.3 The Missing Fundamental Phenomenon

When a tone's fundamental frequency is removed (e.g., by high-pass filtering), listeners still perceive the pitch corresponding to the missing fundamental. This occurs because the auditory system infers pitch from the pattern of harmonics, not from the fundamental alone.

For example, a signal containing only 200, 300, 400, and 500 Hz is perceived as having a pitch of 100 Hz (the common difference between harmonics). This is called **virtual pitch** or **residue pitch** (Schouten, 1940).

This phenomenon is exploited in:
- **Small loudspeakers**: Laptop and phone speakers cannot reproduce frequencies below ~200 Hz, but complex tones with harmonics in the 200-600 Hz range are perceived as having bass fundamentals.
- **Organ pedal stops**: Some organ stops create the illusion of a 16-foot (low C = 32 Hz) pipe by combining two higher-pitched pipes whose difference tone equals the desired fundamental.
- **Sub-bass synthesis**: Layering harmonics of a sub-bass frequency (2nd, 3rd, 4th harmonics) through small speakers creates a perceived low-end weight.

### 8.4 Why Detuned Oscillators Create "Width"

When two oscillators are tuned to nearly the same frequency (e.g., 440 Hz and 440.5 Hz), several perceptual effects combine:

1. **Beating**: The two waves constructively and destructively interfere at a rate equal to the frequency difference (0.5 Hz in this example). This creates a periodic amplitude modulation: the "chorus" effect.

2. **Roughness**: When the detuning exceeds approximately 10-15 Hz (entering the critical band's roughness region), the sound acquires a gritty, thick quality. The Supersaw waveform (Roland JP-8000) exploits this with 7 detuned oscillators.

3. **Stereo width via interaural decorrelation**: When detuned oscillators are panned to different positions (or naturally create slightly different signals at each ear due to room acoustics), the brain perceives a wider source because the two ears receive different phase relationships. The interaural phase difference (IPD) varies continuously due to the beating, preventing the brain from localizing the source to a single point. This is perceived as "width" or "spaciousness."

4. **Spectral filling**: Detuned oscillators produce a thicker spectrum because each oscillator contributes slightly different harmonic frequencies, filling in spectral gaps and creating a denser, more continuous spectral envelope.

### 8.5 Loudness Perception: Fletcher-Munson Curves

The Fletcher-Munson curves (1933, updated by Robinson-Dadson in 1956, standardized as ISO 226:2003) are **equal-loudness contours** showing the SPL required at each frequency to produce the same perceived loudness.

Key observations:
- At low listening levels (~20 phon), the ear is much less sensitive to bass (below 200 Hz) and treble (above 10 kHz). A 40 Hz tone must be approximately 50 dB louder than a 1 kHz tone to sound equally loud.
- At high listening levels (~90 phon), the curves flatten -- the ear perceives all frequencies more equally.
- Maximum sensitivity is around 2-5 kHz (the resonant frequency range of the ear canal), where the ear requires the least SPL.
- There is a secondary sensitivity peak around 13 kHz (the ear canal's second resonance mode).

Implications for synthesis and mixing:
- Bass-heavy synth patches sound thin at low monitoring levels.
- The "loudness" button on hi-fi systems boosts bass and treble to compensate for the ear's reduced sensitivity at low volumes.
- Mixing at approximately 85 dB SPL provides the most neutral frequency balance.

## 9. DIGITAL VS. ANALOG

### 9.1 Aliasing and the Nyquist Theorem

The Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem (1928/1949) states that a continuous signal can be perfectly reconstructed from its samples if and only if the sampling rate is at least twice the highest frequency component:

    f_s >= 2 * f_max

The Nyquist frequency f_N = f_s / 2 is the maximum frequency that can be represented. Frequency components above f_N "fold" back into the range [0, f_N], appearing as spurious lower frequencies:

    f_aliased = |f_original - n * f_s|  (for the n that brings f_aliased into [0, f_N])

For example, at f_s = 44100 Hz (CD quality), a 25000 Hz component aliases to 44100 - 25000 = 19100 Hz -- within the audible range.

In analog-to-digital conversion, an **anti-aliasing filter** (steep lowpass) removes frequencies above f_N before sampling. In digital synthesis, the problem is reversed: the synthesizer must avoid generating harmonics above f_N, which is why bandlimited oscillator techniques (BLIT, minBLEP) are essential.

### 9.2 Why Analog Oscillators Drift and Why That Sounds Good

Analog oscillators use temperature-sensitive components (transistors, capacitors, resistors) whose values shift with environmental conditions and age:

1. **Thermal drift**: Transistor junction voltages change by approximately -2 mV/degree C. In a VCO with a 1V/octave exponential converter, this translates to roughly 0.35% frequency change per degree C -- about 6 cents. A 10-degree temperature swing (cold studio warming up) shifts the pitch by over half a semitone.

2. **Component tolerances**: No two capacitors or resistors are identical. Even with 1% tolerance parts, two "identical" oscillators will differ by several cents.

3. **Power supply variations**: Ripple on the supply voltage directly modulates VCO frequency, creating very slow (60 Hz hum-related) or random micro-variations.

Why this sounds good:
- **Chorus-like thickening**: The random micro-detuning between oscillators creates the same width and richness as intentional detuning, but with a more complex, ever-changing pattern.
- **Avoidance of the "uncanny valley"**: Perfectly stable digital oscillators can sound sterile because their absolute precision is unnatural. No acoustic instrument produces a perfectly stable pitch.
- **Phase decorrelation**: Drifting oscillators never lock into a fixed phase relationship, preventing the cancellation artifacts that can occur with perfectly coherent digital oscillators.

### 9.3 Virtual Analog Modeling Techniques

**Direct form digital filters**: The bilinear transform maps the s-domain (continuous) transfer function to the z-domain (discrete):

    s -> (2/T) * (1 - z^-1) / (1 + z^-1)

where T = 1/f_s. This preserves filter stability but warps the frequency axis:

    f_digital = (f_s / pi) * arctan(pi * f_analog / f_s)

This means a digital filter's cutoff frequency deviates from its analog model at higher frequencies, compressing toward Nyquist.

**Zero-Delay Feedback (ZDF)**: Developed and systematized by Vadim Zavalishin at Native Instruments in his book *The Art of VA Filter Design*. Traditional digital filter implementations introduce a one-sample delay in the feedback path, which causes the filter to respond differently from its analog counterpart, especially at high resonance and high cutoff frequencies. ZDF eliminates this delay by solving the implicit feedback equation at each sample using the trapezoidal integration rule:

    y[n] = (x[n] + state) / (1 + g)
    state = 2 * g * y[n] - state

where g = tan(pi * f_c / f_s). This topology-preserving transform (TPT) maintains the correct relationship between filter stages, producing analog-accurate resonance and self-oscillation behavior.

**Component-level SPICE simulation**: Model each transistor, resistor, and capacitor individually using nodal analysis. Computationally very expensive but can capture the exact nonlinear behavior of a specific circuit. Used in high-end amp simulators (Universal Audio UAD, Analog Obsession).

**Wave Digital Filters (WDF)**: Map circuit elements to digital equivalents using wave variables (incident and reflected waves at each port). Provides modular, component-swappable modeling with guaranteed stability. Used in some modern plugin synths.

### 9.4 Zero-Delay Feedback Filters in Detail

The ZDF approach solves the fundamental problem of digital filter feedback: in a continuous-time circuit, feedback is instantaneous (no propagation delay within the feedback loop). In naive digital implementations, the feedback signal is one sample old, causing:

1. **Resonance frequency shift**: The self-oscillation frequency deviates from the intended cutoff.
2. **Resonance amplitude error**: The peak height at resonance is incorrect.
3. **Instability**: At high cutoff frequencies or high resonance, the one-sample delay can cause the filter to become unstable.

ZDF solves this by reformulating the filter as a system of simultaneous equations (rather than sequential operations) and solving them at each sample. For a one-pole lowpass:

    Analog: y = x - y * (s / omega_c)  [implicit in y]
    Digital ZDF: y[n] = (g * x[n] + s_state) / (1 + g)
    s_state = 2 * y[n] - s_state

For a four-pole (Moog-type) ladder with feedback k:

    Each stage: v[i] = (g * (x[i] - z[i]) + z[i-1]) / (1 + g)
    Feedback: x[0] = input - k * v[4]

This requires solving for v[0]..v[3] simultaneously with the feedback equation -- a 4x4 system that can be reduced to a single scalar equation through algebraic substitution.

## 10. SOUND DESIGN SCIENCE

### 10.1 Velocity-to-Filter Mapping and Acoustic Instrument Behavior

In acoustic instruments, louder playing (higher velocity) produces brighter tones. This is a fundamental physical relationship:

- **Struck strings/percussion**: Harder strikes excite higher harmonics more strongly. The initial displacement profile of the string is more sharply pointed (approaching a step function) with harder strikes, which by Fourier analysis contains more high-frequency content.
- **Bowed strings**: Greater bow pressure and speed increase the "stick-slip" ratio, producing a more sawtooth-like waveform with richer harmonics.
- **Brass**: Louder playing drives the air column into nonlinear oscillation, progressively adding harmonics (a trumpet played at fortissimo has a much brighter spectrum than at pianissimo).

Mapping velocity to filter cutoff in a synthesizer mimics this behavior: higher velocity opens the filter, allowing more harmonics through. The mapping curve should be exponential (or at least concave-up) to match the nonlinear relationship between playing force and brightness in real instruments.

### 10.2 The Science Behind "Warmth"

The perception of "warmth" in audio corresponds to specific measurable spectral characteristics:

**Even harmonics**: Tube amplifiers and tape machines produce predominantly even-order harmonics (2nd, 4th, 6th) when driven into mild saturation. The 2nd harmonic is an octave above the fundamental and reinforces the sense of "fullness" without adding dissonance. The transfer function of a tube (triode) is approximately:

    y = a_1*x + a_2*x^2 + a_3*x^3 + ...

The x^2 term generates the 2nd harmonic; the x^3 term generates the 3rd harmonic. Tubes and transformers tend to have larger a_2 coefficients relative to a_3, favoring even harmonics.

**Tape saturation**: Magnetic tape has a sigmoidal magnetization curve (hysteresis loop). At moderate levels, this produces primarily 3rd harmonic distortion, but the tape's inherent high-frequency rolloff, head bump (a bass boost around 60-100 Hz), and the gentle compression of the saturation curve together produce the characteristic "warm" tape sound. The high-frequency loss (approximately 3-6 dB at 15 kHz) removes harshness.

**Odd harmonics and "coldness"**: Symmetrical clipping (as in transistor push-pull amplifiers) produces predominantly odd harmonics (3rd, 5th, 7th). Odd harmonics at non-octave intervals create less consonant relationships with the fundamental. This is perceived as "harder," "grittier," or "colder."

### 10.3 Why Reverb Creates Emotional Responses

Reverberation triggers emotional responses through multiple evolutionary and psychoacoustic pathways:

1. **Space assessment (survival instinct)**: Our ancestors needed to rapidly assess the size and nature of enclosed spaces (cave, open field, dense forest). Reverberation time (RT60) is the primary acoustic cue for room size. Long reverb suggests large, open spaces (cathedrals, caverns); short reverb suggests small, intimate spaces (closets, burrows). Research shows people perceive small rooms as calmer and safer, while large spaces evoke awe and unease.

2. **Early reflections and proximity**: Early reflections (arriving within 20-80 ms of the direct sound) provide information about the listener's relationship to the source. Minimal early reflections = very close to the source (intimate). Dense early reflections = moderate distance. The ratio of direct-to-reverberant energy (D/R ratio) maps directly onto perceived distance.

3. **Late reverberation and sublimity**: Studies show that longer reverberation times significantly increase perceptions of "sublimity" -- feelings of nostalgia, transcendence, and wonder. This is likely because cathedral-like acoustics are culturally associated with sacred or important spaces.

4. **Temporal smoothing and forgiveness**: Reverb smooths transient imperfections in performance and fills silences between notes, creating a sense of continuity and flowing movement. Dry, unprocessed sound exposes every flaw, creating tension.

### 10.4 Controlled Randomness and the Uncanny Valley of Perfection

Perfectly quantized, perfectly tuned, perfectly timed electronic music can sound lifeless -- an "uncanny valley" effect where the sound is almost-but-not-quite natural, triggering discomfort or disinterest.

The science behind this:

1. **1/f noise (pink noise) in human timing**: Human musical performance exhibits timing variations that follow a 1/f (pink noise) power spectrum -- not random (white noise) and not rigidly periodic. This "fractal" timing creates a sense of organic flow. Studies by Hennig et al. (2011) demonstrated that listeners prefer music with 1/f timing fluctuations over both metronomically perfect and randomly jittered timing.

2. **Microtuning and pitch drift**: Acoustic instruments naturally vary in pitch by several cents during performance. String players' vibrato centers around the target pitch with an approximately 5-7 cent range. This variation keeps the auditory system engaged.

3. **Spectral variation**: Every note on an acoustic instrument has slightly different spectral content due to physical inconsistencies (bow position, embouchure, pick angle). Synthesizer patches that lack this per-note spectral variation sound static by comparison.

4. **Humanization techniques in synthesis**:
   - Add subtle random pitch variation (1-3 cents) per note
   - Apply random velocity variation (5-10%)
   - Introduce timing variation (5-15 ms early/late)
   - Modulate filter cutoff with a slow, irregular LFO
   - Use round-robin sample selection or waveform variation
   - Apply analog-drift simulation to oscillator pitch
